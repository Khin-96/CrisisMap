# CrisisMap – Plain-Language Explanation

## What the project is (in simple words)
CrisisMap is a tool that helps people see where conflict and humanitarian problems are getting worse and where help might be needed soon. It brings together information that is usually scattered across many places and shows it in one clear view. The goal is to help decision-makers act faster and with better information.

In short: it turns messy crisis information into clear early warning signals and easy-to-understand maps, charts, and summaries.

## What problem it solves
In real crises, information comes late and from many sources. By the time reports are combined and understood, the situation may already have changed. This causes delays in response, weak coordination, and resources being sent too late or to the wrong places.

CrisisMap solves this by:
- Bringing different crisis datasets into one place.
- Highlighting unusual spikes in violence or displacement.
- Showing trends so teams can prepare earlier, not just react.

## Who it is for
- Humanitarian teams
- Peacebuilding and security analysts
- Policy researchers and planners
- Coordination offices that need fast, trustworthy summaries

## What it can do (in normal terms)
- Collects conflict and displacement records from reliable sources.
- Organizes the data so it is easy to compare across places and time.
- Shows hot-spots on a map and trends over time.
- Flags early warning signals when patterns look unusual.
- Provides short summaries that can be used in briefs and meetings.

## Why I chose this project
I chose CrisisMap because conflict and displacement are major challenges in the region, and decisions are often made with incomplete or late information. I wanted to build something practical that helps people act earlier, not just report what already happened. It also combines social impact with data skills in a meaningful way, which fits my learning goals.

## Why the project is significant
- It can reduce delays in humanitarian response.
- It improves coordination by giving one shared view of the situation.
- It supports evidence-based planning rather than guesswork.
- It encourages responsible use of data by showing uncertainty and keeping human judgment involved.

## What makes it different from normal reports
Traditional reports are static and slow. CrisisMap is designed to be updated frequently and to highlight changes quickly. It is not just a reporting tool; it is an early-warning and decision-support tool.

## Main data sources used (simple explanation)
- Conflict event data (records of incidents, where they happened, and who was involved).
- Displacement data (where people are moving due to conflict).
- Humanitarian indicators (basic needs and vulnerability signals).

## Scope and limits (what it does and does not do)
What it does:
- Focuses on the Great Lakes Region, especially Eastern DRC.
- Focuses on conflict and displacement trends.
- Supports short-term warning and planning.

What it does not do (in this phase):
- It does not make decisions automatically.
- It does not replace human analysts.
- It does not cover every country or every humanitarian topic yet.

## Ethical and safety considerations
- Sensitive data must be handled carefully.
- Predictions can be misunderstood, so the system shows uncertainty and requires human review.
- The goal is to support decision-makers, not to blame or target communities.

## Likely questions from a lecturer (with simple answers)

1. What is CrisisMap in one sentence?
CrisisMap is a platform that collects crisis information and turns it into clear early-warning insights so people can respond faster.

2. Why is this project needed?
Because crisis data is fragmented and slow. People often respond too late, so a faster and clearer system is necessary.

3. Who benefits from it?
Humanitarian teams, peacebuilding analysts, policy planners, and any organization that needs to act quickly in crisis settings.

4. How is it different from existing dashboards?
Many dashboards only show what already happened. CrisisMap also highlights unusual changes and short-term risks so teams can prepare early.

5. What is the real-world impact?
Better timing and better targeting of aid and peacebuilding efforts, which can reduce harm and improve coordination.

6. What data does it use?
Conflict events, displacement figures, and humanitarian indicators from trusted organizations and datasets.

7. Does it replace human judgment?
No. It supports human decision-makers by giving clearer and faster information, but people still make the final decisions.

8. Is it safe to use sensitive conflict data in one place?
It can be safe if access is controlled and data is handled responsibly. The project includes safeguards and audit records to reduce misuse.

9. What are the limitations?
It is focused on one region, depends on external data quality, and short-term predictions can be uncertain when situations change quickly.

10. How would you improve it in the future?
Add more data sources, support more languages, and improve long-term forecasting while keeping transparency.

11. Why does this matter academically?
It shows how data systems can solve real social problems and connects research, technology, and humanitarian practice.

12. If you had no internet access, could it still work?
Yes. It can keep a local copy of data so analysts can still work offline and sync later.

13. How do you measure success?
By checking if analysts get insights faster, if alerts are accurate, and if decision-making improves compared to manual workflows.

14. What are the risks of wrong predictions?
They can cause false alarms or missed warnings, which is why the system shows uncertainty and relies on human review.

15. Why is this suitable as a group project?
It has clear roles: data collection, analysis, interface design, and testing. It also has social value and real-world relevance.

## Short elevator pitch
CrisisMap turns scattered crisis information into one clear view, showing where risks are rising so response teams can act earlier and more effectively.
