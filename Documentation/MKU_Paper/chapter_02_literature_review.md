## 2.1 Introduction
The review of literature for this project examined the historical evolution and technical foundations of global conflict monitoring systems and predictive analytics. It was not merely a compilation of existing works but a critical assessment of how technological interventions were applied to humanitarian crises on a worldwide scale. The study focused on three primary themes: the transition from regional to global automated monitoring, the role of machine learning in cross-continental social science forecasting, and the impact of interactive 3D visualization on global crisis response. By evaluating these sources, the student sought to establish a theoretical framework that justified the development of the CrisisMap platform as a global intelligence tool.

## 2.2 Historical Context of Global Conflict Monitoring
Early conflict monitoring efforts were characterized by human-centric regional reporting and qualitative analysis. According to Smith (2018), traditional monitoring was geographically limited, relying on field-based observers who documented local events. Smith’s perspective highlighted the "regional silo" problem, where the lack of global standardization prevented the comparison of conflict patterns across different continents. In the context of the modern world, this traditional approach often failed to provide the rapid insights needed for coordinated international aid deployment.

The emergence of global datasets like the Armed Conflict Location & Event Data Project (ACLED) represented a significant shift toward standardized, quantitative monitoring. Raleigh et al. (2010) argued that disaggregated data allowed for more nuanced analysis of global actor behavior. However, a critical examination of Raleigh's work suggested that while the data was robust, it remained largely historical and required significant processing to be useful for real-time forecasting. The student noted that while ACLED provided a global "what" and "where," it did not natively offer the predictive capabilities required for a global early warning system, a gap that CrisisMap intended to fill.

Furthermore, the work of Jones (2020) on international humanitarian data standards emphasized the importance of global interoperability. Jones contended that the lack of standard schemas between regional monitors in Africa, Asia, and Europe created "information fragments" that hindered international response. In the student's view, this fragmentation was a primary driver of global inefficiency. By integrating diverse CSV formats into a unified schema, this project sought to bridge these regional fragments, providing the global intelligence layer that Jones identified as essential for modern international humanitarian efforts.

## 2.3 Technological Advancements in Global Predictive Analytics
The application of machine learning to global conflict forecasting was a relatively recent development in the literature. Brown (2021) explored the use of ensemble models to predict political instability across multiple developing nations. Brown’s research demonstrated that certain features were universal indicators of violence. However, the student observed that Brown’s models were often applied at a national level and did not account for the trans-continental migration of conflict drivers. For a global system, a more adaptive, multi-regional approach was necessary to capture the nuances of different conflict zones.

In a similar vein, Miller and Davis (2022) investigated the role of anomaly detection in identifying outlier events in international conflict data. They argued that statistical spikes in one region could serve as indicators for emerging crises in others. While their technical approach was sound, the student criticized the lack of global visualization tools in their implementation. Miller and Davis produced high-accuracy models that were difficult for international policy-makers to interpret. CrisisMap addressed this by utilizing a 3D globe visualization that made complex international ML outputs intuitive and actionable for a global audience.

The integration of global socio-economic indicators was another key theme. Chen (2019) hypothesized that international displacement trends and global food security levels were leading indicators of regional conflict. Chen’s work provided a compelling case for multi-continental analysis. However, the student noted the challenge of "data variety"—data from Asia often followed different reporting standards than data from Africa. This project mitigated this by implementing an adaptive CSV ingestion engine that standardized global data feeds, a technical solution that Chen’s research suggested but did not fully implement for a global context.
