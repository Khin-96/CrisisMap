## 7.1 Summary of Findings
The CrisisMap project successfully demonstrated that modern technology could be effectively applied to the complex problem of global conflict monitoring. The system met all its primary objectives, providing a real-time, multi-regional platform that improved the intelligence gathering process for Africa, Asia, and Europe. The integration of global CSV datasets and machine learning predictions offered a level of insight that was previously impossible under manual regional reporting. The project proved that a unified, global "intelligence layer" could support humanitarian efforts on a worldwide scale by automating the ingestion and analysis of disparate international data feeds.

## 7.2 Limitations
Despite the success of the project, several limitations were encountered during the global implementation. Time constraints necessitated the prioritization of the most critical conflict zones within the international datasets. Financial limitations prevented the acquisition of proprietary global satellite imagery for ground-truth verification. Additionally, the variety of reporting standards in different regions (Africa vs. Asia vs. Europe) occasionally presented challenges for the automated mapping engine, requiring manual configuration for particularly obscure CSV formats. These factors were documented as areas for future refinement to further enhance the system's global robustness.

## 7.3 Conclusion
In conclusion, CrisisMap represented a significant advancement in international humanitarian technology for the ICMN. By shifting from a fragmented regional reporting cycle to an automated, global predictive cycle, the organization was better equipped to support humanitarian actions across multiple continents. The results of the project tied directly back to the theoretical framework of "Information Ecology," showing that a scalable global platform could harmonize diverse data sources to create a powerful analytical ecosystem. The platform achieved its goal of providing a Silicon Valley-grade intelligence layer for the global peacebuilding sector, setting a new standard for international conflict monitoring.

## 7.4 Recommendations
For future improvements to the global system, the student recommended the following:
1. **Enhanced NLP for Global Reports**: Implementing multi-lingual NLP would allow the system to automatically extract data from situational reports in French, Swahili, Russian, and Arabic.
2. **Global Satellite Integration**: Partnering with international space agencies for low-latency satellite data would improve the verification of remote conflict events.
3. **Federated Learning**: Implementing federated learning would allow different regional offices to train localized models that contribute to a global predictive engine without sharing raw sensitive data.
4. **Blockchain for Data Integrity**: Utilizing blockchain technology to log the provenance of international CSV uploads would ensure the integrity and non-repudiation of the global intelligence feed.
